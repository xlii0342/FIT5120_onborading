Protect Yourself from the Sun

Stay safe with real-time UV tracking and sun protection tips.

Overview

It's great being outdoors, enjoying our beautiful country and climate. But being outdoors means we're exposed to some of the harshest and most dangerous levels of ultraviolet (UV) radiation.

Basking in the sun feels great, but too much UV exposure can leave you with more than just a tan‚Äîit can cause:

  • Sunburn
  • Wrinkles
  • Eye damage
  • Increased risk of skin cancer

The good news? You can enjoy the sunshine safely!

  • Slather on a broad-spectrum sunscreen
  • Rock some stylish UV-blocking sunglasses
  • Throw on a hat for extra protection
  • Stay in the shade during peak hours

Don't let water or snow trick you‚ÄîUV rays bounce off surfaces, making them even stronger. Play it smart, and you can soak up the sun without the damage!

"The best protection is prevention. Shield yourself from the sun!"

Information on Sun Protection

Use sunscreen, wear protective clothing, and seek shade during peak UV hours. Stay informed with real-time UV index updates.

Sunscreen Tips

  • SPF 30+: Use broad-spectrum sunscreen with SPF 30 or higher
  • Apply generously: Use approximately 1 ounce (a shot glass full) to cover your entire body
  • Reapply: Every 2 hours and after swimming or sweating
  • Don't forget: Ears, back of neck, tops of feet, and scalp (if hair is thin)
  • Apply early: Put on sunscreen 15-30 minutes before going outside

Best Protective Clothing

  • UPF Rating: Look for clothes with UPF (Ultraviolet Protection Factor) 50+ rating
  • Fabric: Tightly woven fabrics offer better protection than loosely woven ones
  • Coverage: Long sleeves and pants provide more protection than short sleeves and shorts
  • Hats: Wide-brimmed hats (3+ inches) protect face, ears, and neck better than baseball caps
  • Sunglasses: Choose ones that block 99-100% of UVA and UVB radiation

UV Awareness

  • Peak hours: UV radiation is strongest between 10 AM and 4 PM
  • Cloud cover: Up to 80% of UV rays can penetrate through clouds
  • Reflection: Snow, sand, water, and concrete can reflect UV rays, increasing exposure
  • Altitude: UV exposure increases about 4-5% for every 1,000 feet above sea level
  • Shadow rule: If your shadow is shorter than you, UV exposure is high
